Is Holiday Pay Required by Law?

No federal law requires private-sector employers to pay holiday pay. The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) does not mandate any premium pay for holidays or weekends. However, holiday pay is extremely common as a workplace benefit, and many employers offer it voluntarily or are required to by union contracts.

The only major exception is federal employees, who are entitled to paid holidays and premium pay (typically 2x) for working federal holidays under Title 5 of the US Code.

US Federal Holidays for Premium Pay

The 11 federal holidays in the United States are the most common days when private employers offer holiday premium pay:

  • New Year's Day (January 1)
  • Martin Luther King Jr. Day (3rd Monday in January)
  • Presidents Day (3rd Monday in February)
  • Memorial Day (Last Monday in May)
  • Juneteenth (June 19)
  • Independence Day (July 4)
  • Labor Day (1st Monday in September)
  • Columbus Day / Indigenous Peoples' Day (2nd Monday in October)
  • Veterans Day (November 11)
  • Thanksgiving Day (4th Thursday in November)
  • Christmas Day (December 25)

Common Holiday Pay Multipliers by Industry

  • Retail: Often 1.5x on Thanksgiving, Black Friday, Christmas Eve, Christmas Day
  • Restaurants: Sometimes 1.5x, sometimes regular pay (varies wildly)
  • Healthcare: Often 1.5x or 2x for nurses and aides on major holidays
  • Manufacturing: Often 2x under union contracts
  • Public safety: Police and firefighters often receive 1.5x or 2x by city ordinance
  • Federal employees: 2x guaranteed by federal law for working federal holidays
  • Distribution / warehouse: Often 1.5x to 2x, particularly for late-night holiday shifts

How to Calculate Holiday Pay

The basic formula is the same as overtime:

Holiday Pay = Hourly Rate × Multiplier × Hours Worked

Example 1: Working 8 hours on Christmas at $18/hour with 1.5x multiplier: $18 × 1.5 × 8 = $216.

Example 2: Working 6 hours on Thanksgiving at $25/hour with 2x multiplier: $25 × 2 × 6 = $300.

Example 3: A federal employee working 8 hours on Independence Day at $30/hour: $30 × 2 × 8 = $480.

Holiday Pay vs. Holiday Off Pay

There are two distinct concepts often called "holiday pay":

  • Holiday premium pay: Extra pay (1.5x or 2x) for actually working a holiday
  • Holiday off pay: Getting paid your regular wages for not working on a holiday (paid time off)

Both are voluntary benefits in the private sector. Some employers offer both — you get paid time off if the company is closed, or premium pay if you have to work.

Holiday Pay and Overtime

Hours paid as holiday off pay (when you didn't actually work) typically do not count toward the 40-hour overtime threshold. Only actual hours worked count for overtime calculations under federal law.

For example: If your company is closed on Christmas Day and pays you 8 hours of holiday pay, then you work 40 actual hours the rest of the week, you would not be entitled to overtime even though your paystub shows 48 hours total. However, if you work 40 regular hours plus 8 hours on a holiday, you've worked 48 hours and are owed overtime on those extra 8 hours (in addition to any holiday premium your employer offers).

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my employer have to give me Christmas off?

No. Federal law does not require employers to give any holidays off. Many private companies do, but it's not legally mandated.

Am I entitled to time and a half for working on a holiday?

Only if your employer's policy or union contract requires it. Federal law does not require any premium pay for holidays.

What if a holiday falls on my regular day off?

Most employers either give you another day off ("floating holiday") or pay you for it. This depends entirely on company policy.

Do part-time workers get holiday pay?

Depends on company policy. Some offer prorated holiday pay; others limit it to full-time employees only.

Are religious holidays covered?

Federal employees and many private employers recognize specific religious holidays. Title VII also requires reasonable accommodation for religious observances unless it causes undue hardship.
