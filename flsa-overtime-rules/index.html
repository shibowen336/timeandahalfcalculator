FLSA Overtime Rules: Complete 2026 Guide

The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) is the federal law that establishes minimum wage, overtime pay, recordkeeping, and child labor standards in the United States. Enacted in 1938 and enforced by the US Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division, the FLSA protects more than 143 million American workers. This guide explains everything you need to know about FLSA overtime rules in 2026.

The 40-Hour Workweek Rule

The core FLSA overtime rule is straightforward: non-exempt employees must be paid at 1.5 times their regular rate for every hour worked beyond 40 in a single workweek. This is the federal "time and a half" requirement.

Important details about the 40-hour rule:

  • Overtime is calculated on a workweek basis, not a pay period
  • A workweek is any fixed, recurring 168-hour period (7 consecutive 24-hour periods)
  • Employers cannot average hours across multiple weeks
  • Federal law does not require daily overtime — only weekly
  • Overtime must be paid on the regular payday for the period in which the wages were earned

Exempt vs. Non-Exempt Employees

The FLSA divides employees into two categories. Only non-exempt employees are entitled to overtime pay. To be classified as exempt, an employee must meet ALL three of the following tests:

1. Salary Basis Test

The employee must be paid a predetermined, fixed salary that is not subject to reduction based on the quality or quantity of work performed. They must receive their full salary for any week in which they perform any work.

2. Salary Level Test

As of 2024, exempt employees must earn at least $684 per week ($35,568 per year). The Department of Labor has proposed increases to this threshold, but court challenges have created some uncertainty about the exact figure for 2026. Highly compensated employees (HCE) must earn at least $107,432 per year.

3. Duties Test

The employee's primary duties must fall into one of the recognized exempt categories:

  • Executive exemption: Manages 2+ employees, has hiring/firing authority, and primary duty is management
  • Administrative exemption: Performs office or non-manual work directly related to management or general business operations, exercising independent judgment on significant matters
  • Professional exemption (Learned): Performs work requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning customarily acquired by prolonged specialized education (doctors, lawyers, engineers, architects, teachers)
  • Professional exemption (Creative): Performs work requiring invention, imagination, originality, or talent in a recognized artistic field
  • Computer employee exemption: Computer systems analysts, programmers, software engineers earning at least $27.63/hour or $684/week
  • Outside sales exemption: Primary duty is making sales away from the employer's place of business

Calculating the Regular Rate

Overtime is paid at 1.5 times the "regular rate," which is not always the same as the base hourly wage. The regular rate must include almost all forms of compensation:

  • Hourly wages
  • Non-discretionary bonuses (production, attendance, longevity)
  • Shift differentials
  • Commissions
  • On-call pay

The following payments are excluded from the regular rate calculation:

  • Discretionary bonuses (truly at the employer's discretion)
  • Gifts, holiday bonuses
  • Paid time off (vacation, sick, holiday)
  • Reimbursements for business expenses
  • Employer contributions to benefit plans

How to Calculate Overtime with Bonuses

When non-discretionary bonuses are paid, they must be included in the regular rate. For example:

Sarah earns $20/hour and works 50 hours in a week. She also earns a $100 production bonus that week.

  • Regular pay: $20 × 50 = $1,000
  • Plus bonus: $100
  • Total straight-time pay: $1,100
  • Regular rate: $1,100 ÷ 50 = $22
  • Overtime premium: $22 × 0.5 × 10 = $110
  • Total weekly pay: $1,100 + $110 = $1,210

Common FLSA Violations

The Department of Labor recovers hundreds of millions of dollars in unpaid wages each year. Common FLSA violations include:

  • Misclassification: Incorrectly labeling workers as exempt or as independent contractors
  • Off-the-clock work: Requiring or allowing employees to work without recording the time
  • Comp time substitution: Private employers giving paid time off instead of overtime pay (illegal)
  • Improper averaging: Averaging hours across two weeks to avoid overtime
  • Unpaid prep/cleanup time: Not paying for putting on protective gear, booting up systems, or closing down workstations
  • Excluding bonuses: Failing to include non-discretionary bonuses in the regular rate
  • Improper deductions: Docking exempt employees' pay for partial-day absences (which can destroy the exemption)

How to File an FLSA Complaint

If you believe you're owed unpaid overtime, you have several options:

Option 1: File with the Department of Labor

You can file a confidential wage complaint with the Wage and Hour Division (WHD). The WHD will investigate your employer at no cost to you. Visit dol.gov/agencies/whd or call 1-866-487-9243.

Option 2: File a Private Lawsuit

You can sue your employer directly in federal or state court. Many wage and hour attorneys take these cases on contingency. Under the FLSA, you can recover:

  • All unpaid overtime wages
  • An equal amount in liquidated damages (effectively doubling your recovery)
  • Reasonable attorney's fees and court costs
  • Up to 2 years of back pay (3 years if the violation was willful)

Option 3: Collective Action

If multiple employees were affected by the same violation, you can join or initiate a collective action lawsuit. This is similar to a class action but follows different procedural rules.

FLSA Retaliation Protections

It is illegal for your employer to fire, demote, or discriminate against you for filing a wage complaint or cooperating with a Department of Labor investigation. If retaliation occurs, you can recover additional damages including back pay, front pay, and reinstatement.

State Laws That Exceed FLSA

The FLSA sets a federal minimum standard. States can — and many do — provide greater protections. When state and federal laws differ, the law that benefits the employee more applies. Examples:

  • California, Alaska, Nevada, and Colorado require daily overtime
  • Many states have higher minimum wages than the federal $7.25
  • California has a higher exempt salary threshold than federal law
  • Some states offer longer statutes of limitations for wage claims

Recordkeeping Requirements

Employers are required to maintain records for non-exempt employees including:

  • Personal information (name, address, occupation, sex, birth date if under 19)
  • Time of day and day of week the workweek begins
  • Total hours worked each workday and workweek
  • Basis on which wages are paid
  • Regular hourly rate
  • Total daily or weekly straight-time earnings
  • Total overtime earnings
  • All additions to or deductions from wages
  • Total wages paid each pay period
  • Date of payment and pay period covered

Records must be kept for at least 3 years. Time and pay records must be kept for at least 2 years.
