What is Time and a Half?

Time and a half is an overtime pay rate equal to 1.5 times an employee's regular hourly wage. Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), non-exempt employees in the United States must be paid time and a half for every hour worked beyond 40 hours in a single workweek. This federal labor protection has been in place since 1938 and applies to the majority of hourly workers in the country.

How to Calculate Time and a Half

The formula is straightforward:

Overtime Pay = Hourly Rate × 1.5 × Overtime Hours

Example 1: If you earn $20/hour and work 10 hours of overtime in a week, your overtime pay is: $20 × 1.5 × 10 = $300. Combined with 40 regular hours ($800), your gross weekly pay is $1,100.

Example 2: A nurse earning $35/hour picks up a 12-hour overtime shift. Their overtime rate is $35 × 1.5 = $52.50/hour, and they earn $52.50 × 12 = $630 in overtime pay.

Example 3: A warehouse worker earning $18/hour works 48 hours in a week. Regular pay is $18 × 40 = $720. Overtime pay is $18 × 1.5 × 8 = $216. Total gross pay: $936.

Time and a Half vs. Double Time

These are two different overtime premium rates, and understanding the distinction matters for both workers and employers.

  • Time and a Half (1.5x): Required by federal law for hours worked over 40 per week by non-exempt employees. This is the standard overtime rate nationwide.
  • Double Time (2x): Not required by federal law. Some states — most notably California — require it for hours worked over 12 in a single day or for the 7th consecutive workday. Many employers also voluntarily pay double time for work performed on major holidays.

FLSA Overtime Rules Explained

The Fair Labor Standards Act is the federal law that governs minimum wage, overtime pay, recordkeeping, and child labor standards. Here are the key overtime provisions every worker should know:

  • Overtime kicks in after 40 hours per workweek — federal law does not require daily overtime
  • A workweek is any fixed, recurring 168-hour period (7 consecutive 24-hour periods)
  • Exempt employees (executive, administrative, professional, outside sales, and some computer workers) do not qualify for overtime
  • As of 2024, exempt employees must earn at least $684 per week ($35,568 annually)
  • Some states have stricter rules — always check your state's labor laws
  • Overtime must be paid on the regular payday for the period in which the wages were earned

State Overtime Laws: Daily Overtime Requirements

While federal law only requires weekly overtime after 40 hours, the following states have additional daily overtime requirements that give workers even more protection:

  • California: 1.5x after 8 hours/day, 2x after 12 hours/day. 1.5x for the first 8 hours on the 7th consecutive workday, 2x after 8 hours on the 7th day.
  • Alaska: 1.5x after 8 hours/day for employers with 4+ employees.
  • Nevada: 1.5x after 8 hours/day for workers earning less than 1.5 times the state minimum wage.
  • Colorado: 1.5x after 12 hours/day, or after 12 consecutive hours regardless of workweek start.
  • Oregon: Daily overtime applies in specific industries such as manufacturing and canneries.
  • Kentucky: 1.5x for the 7th consecutive day of work in a week for certain industries.
  • Massachusetts: Follows federal 40-hour rule, but has strict "blue laws" requiring 1.5x on Sundays for certain retail workers (being phased out).

Who Qualifies for Overtime Pay?

The FLSA divides employees into two categories: exempt and non-exempt. Only non-exempt employees are legally entitled to overtime pay.

Non-Exempt Employees (Eligible for Overtime)

  • Most hourly workers
  • Workers earning less than $684 per week ($35,568/year)
  • Blue-collar workers regardless of salary
  • Police, firefighters, paramedics, and other first responders
  • Licensed practical nurses (LPNs) and most other healthcare support staff

Exempt Employees (Not Eligible for Overtime)

  • Executive employees (manage 2+ employees, have hiring authority)
  • Administrative employees (perform office work directly related to business operations)
  • Learned professionals (doctors, lawyers, teachers, engineers)
  • Outside sales employees
  • Certain computer professionals earning at least $27.63/hour
  • Highly compensated employees earning over $107,432/year

Common Overtime Pay Mistakes

Wage theft is unfortunately common. Here are issues to watch for on your paycheck:

  • Misclassification as exempt: Your job title doesn't determine exempt status — your actual duties and pay do.
  • Off-the-clock work: Time spent on prep, cleanup, or mandatory training must generally be paid.
  • Comp time instead of pay: Private-sector employers generally cannot substitute paid time off for overtime wages.
  • Averaging hours across weeks: Overtime must be calculated on a single workweek basis, not averaged.
  • Excluding bonuses from overtime calculation: Non-discretionary bonuses must be included in the regular rate used to compute overtime.
  • Wrong rate for shift differentials: If you earn a higher rate for night shifts, overtime on those hours uses the higher rate.

How Overtime Is Calculated with Multiple Pay Rates

If you work different rates in the same week (say, $15/hour at one job task and $20/hour at another), your overtime rate is based on the weighted average of those rates for that workweek. The formula:

Regular Rate = Total Weekly Pay ÷ Total Hours Worked
Overtime Premium = Regular Rate × 0.5 × Overtime Hours

Some states and union agreements allow you to use the actual rate being worked during the overtime hours instead. Check your contract.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is holiday pay required by law in the US?

No. The FLSA does not require premium pay for holidays. Holiday pay is a benefit offered at the employer's discretion. However, it is standard practice at many companies to offer 1.5x or 2x pay on major federal holidays like Thanksgiving, Christmas, and Independence Day.

Do salaried employees get time and a half?

It depends on their exemption status. Non-exempt salaried employees are entitled to overtime pay. Exempt employees who meet specific duty tests and earn above the FLSA salary threshold ($684/week as of 2024) are not entitled to overtime.

How is overtime calculated for tipped employees?

Tipped employees must have their overtime rate calculated based on the full minimum wage — not the lower cash wage paid by the employer. For example, if the federal minimum wage is $7.25 and the employer pays $2.13 plus tips, overtime is still calculated as $7.25 × 1.5 = $10.88, minus the tip credit.

Can my employer force me to work overtime?

In most states, yes. Unless limited by a union contract or specific state law (like mandatory overtime restrictions for nurses in some states), employers can require overtime and may discipline employees who refuse. They must, however, pay the required overtime premium.

What if my employer doesn't pay me overtime?

You can file a wage complaint with the US Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division (WHD) or your state labor department. You may also file a private lawsuit. Under the FLSA, you can recover back wages plus an equal amount in liquidated damages for up to two years (three years if willful).

Does overtime count toward social security and taxes?

Yes. All overtime pay is subject to federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and state income taxes, just like regular wages. Overtime is not taxed at a higher rate, though it may push you into a higher tax bracket temporarily.

Can I waive my right to overtime pay?

No. Under federal law, employees cannot waive their right to overtime pay. Any agreement to work without overtime pay is invalid and unenforceable.

Is time spent commuting counted as work time?

Generally no, but travel between job sites during the workday is compensable. Also, if you're required to transport tools or equipment, special rules may apply.
