Double Time Pay Calculator

Double time is overtime paid at 2 times your regular hourly rate. While time and a half (1.5x) is required by federal law for hours over 40 per week, double time is not federally mandated. It's required by certain state laws (most notably California) and is commonly offered by employers as a benefit for working holidays or extended shifts.

What Is Double Time Pay?

Double time means earning twice your normal hourly rate for certain hours worked. If your regular rate is $20/hour, double time is $40/hour. The formula is simple:

Double Time Pay = Hourly Rate × 2 × Hours Worked

Example: Working 6 hours of double time at $25/hour = $25 × 2 × 6 = $300.

When Does Double Time Apply?

Unlike time and a half, double time is not a federal requirement. It typically applies in these situations:

  • State law: California requires 2x for hours worked over 12 in a single day
  • 7th consecutive workday: California requires 2x for hours over 8 on the 7th day
  • Union contracts: Many collective bargaining agreements mandate double time for holidays, Sundays, or after a certain number of OT hours
  • Employer policy: Some companies voluntarily pay double time on major holidays (Thanksgiving, Christmas) or for emergency call-outs
  • Specific industries: Construction, healthcare, manufacturing, and public safety often have double time provisions

States That Require Double Time

Only a handful of states have laws that mandate double time pay. These are the most notable:

  • California: 2x for hours over 12 in a single day. 2x for hours over 8 on the 7th consecutive workday.
  • Colorado: No state-mandated 2x, but follows California-style rules for some industries.
  • Federal contracts: Some federal contracts (covered by Davis-Bacon Act or service contracts) may require double time for holiday work.

In every other state, double time is purely a benefit your employer or union has chosen to offer. There is no federal law requiring it under any circumstances.

Double Time vs. Time and a Half

Both are forms of premium pay for overtime, but they serve different purposes:

  • Time and a Half (1.5x): Federally required after 40 hours/week. Standard overtime rate.
  • Double Time (2x): Generally only required by some state laws or union contracts. Reserved for extreme overtime, holidays, or undesirable shifts.

For example, if you work 14 hours in California at $20/hour, the first 8 hours are regular ($160), hours 9–12 are time and a half ($120), and hours 13–14 are double time ($80) — for a total of $360 for that day.

Common Double Time Scenarios

Holiday work: Many companies pay 2x for hours worked on Thanksgiving, Christmas Day, New Year's Day, and Independence Day. This is voluntary unless required by contract.

Emergency call-outs: Some union contracts pay 2x when employees are called in unexpectedly during off-hours.

Extended shifts: Construction and manufacturing workers may receive 2x for hours worked beyond 12 in a single shift.

Sunday work: Some retail union contracts in Massachusetts and other states require Sunday premium pay.

Double Time and Taxes

Double time pay is taxed exactly the same as regular pay. It's subject to federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and any applicable state and local taxes. Receiving double time will not push you into a permanently higher tax bracket — only the actual extra income is taxed at your marginal rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is double time required by federal law?

No. The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) only requires 1.5x for hours over 40 per week. Double time is only required by certain state laws or contracts.

How do I know if I should get double time?

Check your employee handbook, union contract, or state labor law. If you live in California, double time may apply automatically based on your daily hours.

Does double time apply to salaried employees?

Only if they're classified as non-exempt. Most salaried employees are exempt and receive no overtime of any kind, including double time.

Can my employer pay double time instead of time and a half?

Yes — paying more than the legal minimum is always allowed. Some employers offer double time as a recruiting or retention incentive.
