Texas Overtime Calculator

Texas does not have its own state overtime law. Instead, Texas follows the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), which requires 1.5 times the regular rate for non-exempt employees working more than 40 hours in a workweek. This means Texas workers have fewer protections than workers in California or New York, but the federal rules still apply. This calculator helps you determine your Texas overtime pay.

Texas Overtime Law Overview

The Texas Labor Code does not establish a state-level overtime requirement. Instead, Texas defers entirely to the federal FLSA for overtime protections. This means:

  • Overtime is 1.5x the regular rate
  • It kicks in after 40 hours per workweek — not per day
  • There is no daily overtime requirement in Texas
  • There is no double time requirement in Texas (unless in a contract)
  • Exempt employees must meet the federal $684/week threshold

Texas Minimum Wage

Texas uses the federal minimum wage of $7.25/hour. Texas has not raised its minimum wage above the federal level, making it one of 20 states that stick with $7.25. Some Texas cities have attempted to pass local minimum wage increases, but state law generally preempts these efforts for private employers.

Texas Exempt Employee Rules

Because Texas follows federal FLSA, the exempt employee rules are the federal ones. To be exempt from overtime in Texas, an employee must:

  • Earn at least $684 per week ($35,568/year) — the federal threshold
  • Be paid on a salary basis
  • Have duties that qualify under executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales exemptions

Texas Examples: Calculating Overtime

Example 1: Jake is a warehouse worker in Houston earning $18/hour. He works 52 hours in a week:

  • Regular pay: $18 × 40 = $720
  • Overtime: $18 × 1.5 × 12 = $324
  • Total: $1,044

Example 2: Maria is an oil field worker in Midland earning $28/hour. She works 60 hours:

  • Regular pay: $28 × 40 = $1,120
  • Overtime: $28 × 1.5 × 20 = $840
  • Total: $1,960

Example 3: Ravi works a 14-hour shift on one day in Dallas at $25/hour, then only works 20 more hours the rest of the week (total 34 hours):

  • Regular pay: $25 × 34 = $850
  • No overtime — total hours are under 40
  • Unlike California, Texas does not require daily overtime for the 14-hour day
  • Total: $850

Texas Workforce Commission (TWC)

The Texas Workforce Commission handles unpaid wage complaints in Texas. If your employer fails to pay overtime, you have two options:

  • File with TWC: Submit a wage claim within 180 days of when the wages were due. TWC can investigate and order payment.
  • File an FLSA complaint: Contact the US Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division for federal FLSA violations. You can recover unpaid wages plus liquidated damages.
  • Private lawsuit: You can sue in federal or Texas state court, potentially recovering double damages and attorney's fees.

Texas Right-to-Work State

Texas is a "right to work" state, meaning employees cannot be required to join a union as a condition of employment. This affects overtime indirectly — fewer Texas workers are covered by collective bargaining agreements that might provide double time or other premium pay above the federal minimum.

Texas Public Employee Overtime

Public employees (state, county, and municipal workers) in Texas are generally covered by the FLSA, but public-sector employers can offer "compensatory time off" (comp time) in lieu of overtime pay — up to 240 hours for most employees and 480 hours for public safety workers. Private employers in Texas cannot offer comp time; they must pay cash overtime.

Common Texas Industries and Overtime

  • Oil and gas: Long shifts are common. Despite high pay, many oil field workers are misclassified as exempt when they should receive overtime.
  • Construction: Laborers are typically non-exempt and entitled to overtime.
  • Trucking: Interstate drivers are often exempt under the Motor Carrier Act exemption.
  • Healthcare: Nurses, techs, and aides are typically non-exempt.
  • Tech: Software developers meeting the computer professional exemption may be exempt.

Texas Overtime FAQ

Does Texas have daily overtime?

No. Texas follows federal FLSA, which only requires weekly overtime after 40 hours. You can work a 16-hour day and not receive overtime if your weekly total stays under 40.

Does Texas require double time?

No. No Texas or federal law requires double time. Your employer may offer it by policy or contract, but it's not legally required.

What is the Texas overtime statute of limitations?

TWC wage claims must be filed within 180 days. Federal FLSA claims must be filed within 2 years (3 years if willful). A private lawsuit may be available for longer periods.

Can my Texas employer give me comp time instead of overtime pay?

Only if you are a public-sector employee. Private-sector Texas employers cannot substitute comp time for cash overtime.

Are salaried Texas workers entitled to overtime?

Only if they are non-exempt. If a salaried employee earns less than $684/week or doesn't meet the duties test, they are entitled to overtime under federal law.
